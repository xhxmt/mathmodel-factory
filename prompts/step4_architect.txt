DISCARD DISCIPLINE: Be explicit about findings that should stay out of the paper's core claim set because they are too weak, too sample-specific, too identification-fragile, too mechanistically speculative, or too off-argument. FOCUS DISCIPLINE:
- Propose one focused claim, not a tour of everything in the project.
- The body of the paper should be built from a small number of results that work together.
- Robustness tests and elaborations should deepen the chosen claim, not create parallel stories.
- If a result is interesting but not needed to build up the paper, put it in the appendix or discard it.
- A proposal that tries to carry too many central findings should be treated as weakly disciplined.

Based on everything you've read, propose a paper. Write your proposal to `paper_map___N__.md` with these sections:

## Core Argument
State the paper's central claim in 3–4 sentences. This must be a substantive argument — what the paper *claims*, not what it *studies*. A reader should be able to disagree with this claim.

## Contribution
Why is this argument important or at odds with what we would predict? What does this paper change about how we understand the topic? Be specific about which prior findings or expectations this challenges.

## Identification Strategy
What is the primary empirical strategy for the paper you're proposing? How strong is the causal identification? What are the main threats to identification and how does the evidence address them? If you are proposing a different identification strategy than the one in findings_brief.md, explain why it is superior.

## Paper Map
For each section of the paper (Introduction, Background, Data, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion, Appendix), write 3–5 sentences describing what it covers and which specific findings, figures, and tables it draws on. This should read as a detailed outline that a writing agent could follow.

## Central Findings (Body of Paper)
List the specific results, figures, and tables that belong in the main body. Keep this focused. For each, state: (a) what it shows, with specific numbers from the logs/tables, and (b) why it is central to the argument - how it advances the claim. Do not include results that are merely interesting side notes.

## Supporting Findings (Appendix)
List results, figures, and tables that belong in the appendix (robustness checks, secondary heterogeneity, supplementary analyses). For each, briefly note what it supports.

## Findings to Discard
List any results from the extensions or initial analyses that do not fit the proposed argument and should be dropped. Be concrete: name the result, the source table/figure/do-file if possible, and whether it is being dropped because of weak identification, weak precision, sample misalignment, external-validity limits, or narrative irrelevance. Include tempting results that would sound good in a seminar but would overstate the paper if they resurfaced in the main text.

## Potential Weaknesses
What would a skeptical reviewer challenge about this argument and evidence? Be honest — identify the 2–3 most serious objections and how (or whether) the current evidence addresses them.
