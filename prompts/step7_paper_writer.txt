**Section requirements:**

**Introduction** (~1,500 words):
- Open with clear statement of the theoretical question and stakes of the paper. By the end of the first paragraph, the reader should know what the current state of understanding of the paper's topic is.
- The second paragraph should state the paper's core argument at a conceptual level. State the paper's argument — what the paper claims, not just what it studies. The argument should be expressible in 3–4 sentences.
- Do NOT say the argument is "surprising in several ways" or enumerate multiple dimensions of surprise. Instead, build the argument carefully, then focus the contribution statement on its most important audience — the specific scholarly debate or practical community whose understanding this paper changes. Frame it as an intervention in existing debates, not as filling a gap.
- Often a well-chosen illustrative example makes the research question concrete and vivid. **CRITICAL: Never fabricate examples, quotes, vignettes, or composite portraits.** Every illustrative example must come from a real, citable source. First check whether the data itself contains usable examples. If not, use a WebSearch to find a real news article, court case, government report, or other published source. Cite the source. If you cannot find a suitable real example, no problem; don't include it.
- Preview the empirical strategy and key findings in qualitative terms. Do NOT enumerate every finding with specific numbers in the introduction. Describe the broad shape of what the evidence shows.
- Conclude the introduction with the big picture takeaway of the argument.
- The introduction should read as a sustained argument, not as a summary of the paper's contents. A reader who stops after the introduction should understand what the paper claims and why it matters, not just what it does.

**Background** (~2,000 words):
- Title this section something substantive/conceptual, not just 'background'.
- This is the place to develop the conceptual argument that will be assessed in the empirical analysis.
- Do NOT write a literature review organized by subfield or tradition (e.g., one subsection on populism, another on professions, another on credentialism). Instead, organize the background around the paper's own argument. Each paragraph or subsection should advance one step of the argument, citing prior work as evidence, foils, or building blocks along the way.
- Prior research should be used architecturally: each citation cluster motivates a mechanism, an alternative explanation, or a testable contrast.
- Do NOT write about "literatures" as such; write about the world and ideas, and support claims with prior work.
- The conceptual argument and findings MUST align tightly. If they don't, change the argument to match what you actually found.
- The theory proposed in the paper should be presented in contrast to alternative approaches when relevant.
- Often a conceptual tikz diagram can be helpful for focusing the reader on the mechanics of the argument

**Data** (~1,000 words):
- Describe data sources, sample construction, key variables
- Use `data_context.md` when explaining the producer, reporting regime, questionnaire/filing logic, or institutional meaning of the main data. Do not describe a measure more strongly than the documentation supports.
- Use `sample_support.md` as the authoritative source for the universe, matched sample, main estimating sample, treated clusters, subgroup shares, and any sample-path caveats. Do not make the reader infer these from scattered table notes.
- **CRITICAL: Data provenance.** Any external data that was downloaded during the analysis (election results, Census/ACS demographics, geographic crosswalks, price indices, policy databases, etc.) must be footnoted in the Data section with the exact source and provenance: the repository or agency name, the specific URL or dataset identifier, the date accessed, and the version or vintage if applicable. Do not describe downloaded data vaguely (e.g., "county-level election results from a public repository"). Instead, give the precise citation (e.g., "County-level presidential election returns from the MIT Election Data and Science Lab, Harvard Dataverse, doi:10.7910/DVN/VOQCHQ, accessed March 2026"). This applies to every external dataset, not just the primary data.
- Include a summary statistics table
- Reference descriptive figures

**Methods** (~800 words):
- Describe the empirical strategy clearly
- Justify identification assumptions
- Discuss potential threats
- Use `data_context.md` to disclose any producer-side rules, reporting thresholds, redesigns, or documentation caveats that affect interpretation of the design.
- If the main estimating sample differs materially from the raw universe, if treatment support is thin, or if a headline result relies on a special subsample, disclose that cleanly using `sample_support.md` rather than burying it.

**Results** (~1,750 words):
- Present main findings systematically
- Each set of results should be linked back to the paper's overall argument. Do not just describe what the tables show — explain what each finding means for the claim the paper is making.
- **CRITICAL**: Every number you report in the text must be checked TWICE against the actual tables and figures. Read the log files or table files and verify each number before writing it. If a number doesn't match, fix it.
- Reference all tables and figures by label
- Discuss economic/substantive magnitude, not just statistical significance

**Robustness checks** (~500 words):
- Briefly discuss robustness checks, referencing back to the main results they test and to the appendix section that includes the actual tables and figures here and a more thorough discussion
- Note the specific objection or counter-argument that each robustness test addresses

**Discussion** (~800 words):
- Interpret the results and connect to broader literature and theory
- When a potential counterargument exists, confront it directly with evidence rather than listing it as a limitation. The Discussion should strengthen the argument, not qualify it.
- A brief paragraph at the very end acknowledging the most important remaining limitations is fine, but keep it short and do not let it undermine the paper's claims. The dominant tone should be confident interpretation, not hedging.
- Do NOT resurrect findings banned in `dropped_findings.md`. If a result was discarded as tempting-but-weak, keep it out of the Discussion unless later steps explicitly clear it.

**Conclusion** (~500 words):
- Summarize contributions
- Policy implications if relevant
- Future directions
- Do NOT mention discarded findings in the Conclusion.

**Appendix** (after bibliography):
- The paper MUST include an appendix. Material that is not necessary for the average reader to follow the main argument belongs here, not in the body.
- **Robustness results**: All robustness checks and alternative specifications go in the appendix. The body should mention that robustness tests were conducted and briefly summarize the takeaway (e.g., "Results are robust to alternative specifications; see Appendix Table A3"), but the actual tables and detailed discussion belong in the appendix.
- **Background results**: Supplementary descriptive analyses, additional heterogeneity breakdowns, or secondary findings that support but are not central to the main story go in the appendix.
- **Detailed data/methods background**: Extended variable definitions, detailed sample construction steps, additional summary statistics, or methodological derivations that would slow down the body of the paper for a general reader belong in the appendix.
- Include an appendix table or subsection built from `sample_support.md` whenever the sample path, treated clusters, subgroup alignment, or match rates are not obvious from the main text.
- The appendix should be organized with its own numbered tables (Table A1, A2, ...) and figures (Figure A1, A2, ...) using labels like `tab:a1`, `fig:a1`. Use `\setcounter{table}{0}\renewcommand{\thetable}{A\arabic{table}}` and the same for figures at the start of the appendix.
- The body of the paper should be streamlined for the main argument. When in doubt about whether something belongs in the body or appendix, put it in the appendix and reference it from the body.

**Bibliography** — generate `references.bib`:
- Use ONLY references you are confident actually exist (real authors, real journals, approximately correct years)
- Include foundational works and recent contributions
- **CRITICAL: Do NOT use the `url` field in any BibTeX entry.** The `bibliography.bst` file has a bug that causes fatal errors when a `url` field is present. Simply omit the `url` field from every entry. Use correct entry types (`@book` for books, `@techreport` for working papers and reports, `@misc` for legislation, `@article` for journal articles). Every entry must have at minimum: `author`, `title`, and `year`, plus type-specific required fields (`journal` for `@article`, `publisher` for `@book`, `institution` for `@techreport`).
